## Essay Question

**Topic:** Causes of the First World War

**Question:** "To what extent was the alliance system the primary cause of the outbreak of the First World War in 1914? In your answer, consider the relative importance of nationalism, imperialism, and the arms race."

---

## Scoring Rubric

| Criteria | **1 - Beginning** | **2 - Developing** | **3 - Proficient** | **4 - Exemplary** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Causation & Complexity** | Lists causes without explaining how they contributed to war. | Explains one or two causes in isolation with limited connection to the outbreak. | Analyses multiple causes (alliances, nationalism, imperialism, arms race) and shows how they interconnected. | Constructs a hierarchy of causes; argues convincingly why some factors were structural and others were triggers, demonstrating historiographical awareness. |
| **Focus on the Question** | Does not address the "to what extent" framing; no verdict on alliances. | Touches on the alliance system but treats all causes as equally significant without prioritising. | Addresses the centrality of the alliance system and explicitly weighs it against other causes. | Nuanced judgement on the relative weight of the alliance system; conclusion is proportionate and supported by the argument throughout. |
| **Evidence & Specificity** | Vague generalisations with no specific events, dates, or figures. | Some specific examples (e.g., Sarajevo, Schlieffen Plan) but used superficially. | Accurate use of specific evidence (e.g., Triple Entente vs. Triple Alliance, July Crisis, Weltpolitik) to substantiate claims. | Rich, well-chosen evidence deployed analytically; student uses historians' interpretations (e.g., Fischer debate, revisionist views) to deepen argument. |
| **Structure & Argument** | No coherent argument; narrative retelling rather than analysis. | Argument is present but paragraphs drift from the question. | Clear PEEL structure; each paragraph advances the central argument with a clear link back to the question. | Masterfully structured; argument builds logically, counter-arguments are acknowledged and rebutted, and the conclusion synthesises rather than repeats. |

---

### Scoring Guide
* **13–16 Points:** High Distinction — Sophisticated, well-evidenced historical argument with clear evaluative judgement.
* **9–12 Points:** Pass/Credit — Competent analysis covering multiple causes with some prioritisation.
* **5–8 Points:** Developing — Mostly narrative; causes identified but not rigorously analysed.
* **0–4 Points:** Needs Improvement — Fundamental misunderstanding of the period or the question.
